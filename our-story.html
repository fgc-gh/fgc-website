Vision

We start from the body, not the argument. Before the philosophy, there is the sensation: cold water moving over your hand, the specific weight of old-growth air, the sound of a creek in the dark carrying information you can almost but not quite read. The body knows something the argument is still trying to catch up to — that the boundary between self and world is not where we have been taught it is. That what we call "the environment" is not a context for human life but its literal substance. That ecological destruction is not harm to something outside you. It is self-harm.

This is not a metaphor. The oxygen in your blood was made by the same photosynthetic process running in the trees. The water in your cells fell as rain over a watershed. Your nervous system shares its basic architecture with the crow watching from the fence post — the same electrochemical pulse, refined over five hundred million years of shared history. When a forest is cleared, something that was helping make you possible is gone. When a salmon run collapses, a link in a chain that connects you, metabolically and evolutionarily, to something very old is severed.

Paying attention to a place long enough, you start to feel this rather than think it. The creek stops being a feature of the landscape and starts being a presence. The spotted owl hunting the old growth at night stops being a protected species and starts being a neighbor in a neighborhood you also inhabit. That shift — from extraction to participation, from ownership to membership — is what we are working toward. Not as an abstraction but as the practical ground from which everything else follows.

Fertile Ground Conservancy is a small organization. We file comments on timber sales. We hold fifteen acres in the Klamath watershed. We give grassroots ecological initiatives a place to operate. These are not grand gestures. They are the particular work that happens to be available to us, in the time available to us, with the capacity we have. We do not always know if it is enough. We do it anyway.

Approach

We work at three points of leverage: defending wild places from the administrative and legal mechanisms that threaten them, sponsoring grassroots ecological work that needs an organizational home, and holding specific land in permanent trust for the communities of life who inhabit it. These are not separate programs — they are coordinated expressions of the same underlying commitment. Filing a comment on a federal timber sale, sponsoring a mining resistance campaign, and protecting a Coho salmon watershed are all acts of loyalty to the living world against the logic of extraction.

Our biocentric orientation means we take seriously the interests and inherent worth of nonhuman beings — not as a rhetorical gesture but as a practical constraint on how we work. We do not pursue conservation that trades ecological integrity for political palatability. We do not sponsor projects that compromise on the fundamental dignity of the living world.

We are a small organization. We do not pretend otherwise. What we offer is not scale but alignment — a fiscal and legal home for work that cannot find a home elsewhere, and a commitment to holding land in perpetuity for the benefit of the communities of life that inhabit it.

Theory of Change

Here is what we actually know about how this works: a scoping notice is posted. The comment window is thirty days. If no one submits substantive comments grounded in ecological science and applicable law, the administrative record is thin and the agency proceeds. If someone does, the record is thicker, the analysis is harder to finesse, and — sometimes — the outcome is different. We have seen this happen. We have also seen it not happen. The uncertainty is not a reason to stop. It is the condition of the work.

Permanent land protection operates differently. Once a parcel is in conservation ownership, with deed restrictions recorded at the county, the calculation changes regardless of what happens politically or economically above it. That land cannot be sold for development. The salmon don't need to make that argument again. The cedar doesn't need a new administration to stay standing. Permanence is the point.

Grassroots movements that don't have organizational infrastructure — nonprofit status, grant eligibility, financial accounts, compliance capacity — burn through their energy on administration rather than advocacy. Many of them collapse before they win anything. Fiscal sponsorship lowers that barrier. It does not guarantee success. It removes one of the most common causes of failure.

These three things together — administrative presence, permanent protection, organizational infrastructure — are not a theory so much as a practice. They are what we have learned to do with the capacity we have, in the time available to us, on behalf of places and species that cannot make these arguments for themselves. We do not expect to see the full results. We are not sure anyone will. We do it anyway because the alternative is to watch it happen and have done nothing.

History
  • 2009

    Founded in Bellingham, Washington as a grassroots environmental education nonprofit. Began organizing in the community against the operation of Tar Sands pipelines beneath the city.

  • 2011–2012

    Organized a speaking tour across the West Coast, building coalitions across Indigenous, feminist, anti-war, and environmental movements.

  • 2010–2014

    Secured $250,000 in funding to organize the Earth At Risk conference series in the San Francisco Bay Area. Events featured Arundhati Roy, Alice Walker, Vandana Shiva, Chris Hedges, Dominique Christina, and other prominent social and ecological justice advocates. Over 1,000 attendees across the series.

  • 2009–2019

    Fiscally sponsored seven grassroots ecological initiatives, including Protect Thacker Pass, the Pinyon-Juniper Alliance, Rural Watch Africa Initiative, Viveros Farm, Mountain and Waters Alliance, the Sovereign Housing Project for the Ahousaht First Nation, and the Good Grief Group.

  • 2019

    Hosted Indigenous and Women of Color Rise in Seattle, Washington — an event focused on male violence, sexual exploitation, objectification, and racialization.

  • 2024

    Transitioned to a land conservancy model with a focus on conserving ecologically and culturally significant lands in the western United States, alongside continued fiscal sponsorship of aligned grassroots initiatives.

  • 2025

    Took ownership of our first stewarded parcel — 15 acres in the Elk Creek watershed, Del Norte County, California. Habitat for endangered Coho salmon, Northern Spotted Owl, Port Orford cedar, Pacific Giant salamander, Roosevelt elk, cougar, and black bear.

  • 2026

    Centered ecodefense as the primary organizational mission. Launched the Conservation Defense Initiative (CDI), an AI-assisted infrastructure for monitoring, classifying, and intervening in administrative actions threatening wild places. First pilot intervention: BLM Western Oregon Resource Management Plan revision.

Board & Staff

  • Dillon Thomson

    Executive Director

    Dillon is a co-founding member of Fertile Ground Conservancy and its first Executive Director. He grew up in the damp green of occupied Chehalis land — part of what is now known as western Washington State — a land plagued by clearcuts and dripping with wild beauty. It was seeing this beauty and wildness continually eaten away by new industry and development that made Dillon realize the dominant economic system is fundamentally at odds with the natural world. Later, he found a home for his love and grief in the deep ecology and conservation movements.

  • Max Wilbert

    Board President

    Max Wilbert is a writer and biocentric community organizer who has been part of grassroots political work for 20 years. He is the founder of Protect Thacker Pass and the author of two books, most recently Bright Green Lies: How The Environmental Movement Lost Its Way and What We Can Do About It (Monkfish, 2021). His work has been featured in the New York Times, CNN, NPR, Le Monde, BBC, and elsewhere.

  • Saba Malik

    Secretary

    Saba Malik has dedicated her adult life to environmental activism and community organizing. She has spoken at leading conferences including PIELC and Earth At Risk, sharing insights on ecological justice and the urgent need for systemic change. Currently, Saba focuses on supporting at-risk youth in the Bay Area through material assistance and mentorship. An early member of Fertile Ground, she brings decades of experience, passion, and vision to building a more just and sustainable future.

  • Rahman Malik

    Treasurer

    Rahman is a visionary entrepreneur who chose to diverge from the traditional path by forgoing college to pursue his dreams as a business owner. Fertile Ground Conservancy represents his passion for protecting wild nature, where he finds solace and thrives amidst the serenity of the wilderness.

We operate on the unceded territories of the Tolowa, Yurok, Karuk, and other Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Coast. These nations have lived in constitutive relationship with this land for thousands of years — not managing it, not owning it, but belonging to it in a way that shaped their language, governance, ceremony, and body of knowledge. What FGC is trying to practice, they have been practicing since long before conservation was a word. We are working to act in a manner consistent with that prior claim, and we fall short of it regularly. We name this not as ceremony but as accountability.
