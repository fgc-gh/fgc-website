Elk Creek runs through the old-growth redwood country of Del Norte County, where the Klamath Mountains descend to meet the Pacific. This is one of the most biologically rich and least disturbed landscapes remaining in California — a refuge that has survived the logging era by circumstance more than by protection, and that now faces pressure from all sides.

Our 15-acre parcel sits within the lower Elk Creek watershed, in the riparian zone where the forest meets the water. This is the interface that matters most: the shaded creek banks that keep water temperatures cold enough for Coho salmon, the root systems that hold the soil against the current, the fallen logs that create the complex structure juvenile salmon need to survive their first winter.

We hold this land the way you hold a wound — with attention, with as much restraint as we can manage, and with the understanding that healing is something the body does, not something you do to it. Elk Creek is not sick because of anything that happened here. It is under pressure from what is happening everywhere around it. Our role is to keep that pressure off the wound long enough for the body's own processes to work.

Who lives here

Elk Creek is not a remote wilderness. It runs along the edge of a small California coastal town, through second-growth forest recovering from the logging era, past ridgelines where Port Orford cedar still stands on the steep ground that was too difficult to cut. It is a working watershed — pressed on all sides, asked to absorb more than it should.

And it is holding. Coho salmon still return here in the fall, navigating the same cold tributaries their ancestors navigated for thousands of years. A Spotted Owl hunts the old-growth pockets at night. The Pacific Giant salamander, which requires cold clean water and undisturbed streambed gravel, is still present — which means the creek is still doing what a creek is supposed to do.

These presences are not incidental. They are the measure of the place. As long as the salmon are returning and the salamander is here, Elk Creek is functioning as a community. Our job is to keep the wound from getting worse. The salmon returning, the salamander present in the gravel — these are signs the body is still functioning. They are what we are here to defend.

Why Elk Creek

The Klamath-Siskiyou region is one of the most biologically significant temperate landscapes on Earth. Where the Cascade Range meets the Coast Ranges and the Sierra Nevada, the collision of geology, climate, and elevation has produced a mosaic of plant communities found nowhere else — more conifer species than any comparable area on the continent, a center of endemism for flowering plants, and a refuge for species that survived the ice ages in this corner of the coast while much of the continent was buried under glaciers. Conservation biologists rank it among North America's biodiversity hotspots. Elk Creek sits inside this region, in the coastal fringe where maritime fog, old-growth redwood, and cold mountain streams converge.

Coho salmon in the Klamath Mountain region are among the most imperiled populations on the West Coast. Their survival depends on the integrity of entire watersheds — cold water, intact riparian canopy, minimal sediment, unobstructed passage. This is not a list of habitat requirements. It is a description of the conditions under which a particular body can exist. Every parcel that stays forested and undisturbed is a portion of the creek's circulatory system kept intact. Every spawning closure — the seasonal restriction on human disturbance during the weeks when salmon are completing their cycle — is wound-rest. The body cannot heal if it cannot stop being injured.

Elk Creek is also a stronghold for Port Orford cedar — one of the last populations in California not yet reached by Phytophthora lateralis, the root pathogen that has killed cedars across most of their range. The cedar has no evolutionary relationship with this pathogen. It arrived on equipment, on boot treads, in soil moved by roads. The cedar's immune system has no answer for it. Ours does: the biosecurity protocol we follow on this parcel — restricted vehicle access, boot cleaning at the boundary, no soil disturbance in the drainage — is the watershed's immune response, running through human behavior. We are part of the body here. The pathogen is not. Keeping this parcel off-limits to the vectors that carry it is not management. It is the body recognizing and excluding what does not belong.

This land does not need management. It needs the injuries to stop. Our role is to hold the legal and financial infrastructure that keeps it out of harm's way — permanently, and regardless of what political or economic conditions follow.

We operate on the unceded territories of the Tolowa, Yurok, Karuk, and other Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Coast. These nations have lived in constitutive relationship with this land for thousands of years — not managing it, not owning it, but belonging to it in a way that shaped their language, governance, ceremony, and body of knowledge. What FGC is trying to practice, they have been practicing since long before conservation was a word. We are working to act in a manner consistent with that prior claim, and we fall short of it regularly. We name this not as ceremony but as accountability.
